Huawei Summer Training: What I Learned

Career Industry

A month with Huawei's AI team showed me how enterprise-scale deep learning differs from academic projects. The XJTLU Summer PDP Training was my first exposure to industry-level AI development, and it reshaped my understanding of what it means to build AI systems that actually work in the real world.

Scale of Operations

The first thing that impressed me was the sheer scale. At university, we work with datasets that fit on a single GPU. At Huawei, they're dealing with petabytes of data across distributed systems. The engineering challenges are completely different.

I learned about data pipelines that process millions of samples per hour, model serving infrastructure that handles thousands of requests per second, and monitoring systems that track every aspect of model performance in production.

In academia, we optimize for benchmark scores. In industry, we optimize for everything else too.

Case Studies: Google, Microsoft, Huawei

One of the most valuable parts of the training was analyzing how world-class companies approach AI. We studied case studies from Google, Microsoft, and Huawei itself, looking at how they architect their systems, handle edge cases, and iterate on their models.

What struck me was how much effort goes into the "boring" parts: data quality, monitoring, testing, documentation. These aren't glamorous topics, but they're often the difference between a prototype and a production system.

The Human Element

Perhaps surprisingly, the training also emphasized the human element of AI development. How do you communicate with stakeholders who don't have technical backgrounds? How do you prioritize when everything seems urgent? How do you build sustainable practices that don't burn out the team?

These soft skills sometimes matter more than technical prowess. A brilliant model that can't be explained to decision-makers will never ship.

Bringing It Back to Research

I returned to my academic work with a new perspective. I started thinking more about deployment constraints from the beginning of projects. I became more rigorous about documentation and reproducibility. I began asking "who will use this?" and "how will it be maintained?" earlier in the research process.

The training didn't make me want to abandon academia for industry—if anything, it reinforced my appreciation for the freedom to explore fundamental questions. But it did help me build bridges between the two worlds, which I think makes me a better researcher.
