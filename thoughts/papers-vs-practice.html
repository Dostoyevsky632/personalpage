The Gap Between Papers and Practice

Industry Reflection

After months of internship work on video processing pipelines, I've gained new perspective on the distance between academic metrics and production systems. The gap is wider than I ever imagined, and navigating it has been one of the most valuable learning experiences of my career so far.

In papers, we optimize for Dice scores and mIoU. We celebrate improvements of 0.5% on benchmark datasets. We write about "state-of-the-art" results that may never see the light of a production environment.

What Production Actually Cares About

In production, the priorities are fundamentally different:

  • Latency — A model that takes 2 seconds to run might be unusable, regardless of accuracy
  • Memory footprint — Can it run on the target hardware?
  • Edge cases — What happens with unusual inputs that never appear in benchmark datasets?
  • User experience — Does the output actually help the user accomplish their goal?
A model with 2% lower accuracy but 10x faster inference often wins in real applications.

This was a hard pill to swallow at first. I had spent so much time optimizing for benchmark performance that I initially resisted the idea that those metrics might not matter as much as I thought.

The "Good Enough" Threshold

In many production scenarios, there's a "good enough" threshold for accuracy. Once you cross it, further improvements yield diminishing returns for the user. But improvements in latency, reliability, or ease of deployment can make a huge difference.

During my internship at Petlibro, I saw this firsthand. We had a detection model that was technically less accurate than the state-of-the-art, but it ran in real-time on edge devices. That practical constraint was non-negotiable—the most accurate model in the world would be useless if it couldn't process video fast enough.

Changing How I Approach Research

This experience has shaped how I approach research now. When I work on a new model, I always ask: "but will it deploy?" This doesn't mean abandoning fundamental research, but it does mean keeping practical constraints in mind from the beginning.

I've also learned to appreciate the engineering side of ML more. Writing clean, efficient code isn't just about elegance—it directly impacts whether your research can have real-world impact. The gap between a notebook prototype and a production system is vast, and bridging it is a skill in itself.
