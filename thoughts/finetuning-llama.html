First Impressions of Fine-tuning LLaMA

LLMs TCM

The MedGPT-Quiz project was my first deep dive into large language models. Working with Prof. Jianwei Lu's team at Shanghai University of Traditional Chinese Medicine, we fine-tuned LLaMA to answer questions about Traditional Chinese Medicine. The experience fundamentally changed how I think about AI.

Coming from a computer vision background, I had certain expectations about how model training would work. LLMs shattered many of those assumptions.

Scale Changes Everything

The first thing that struck me was the sheer scale. We were working with billions of parameters. Training runs took days, not hours. A single hyperparameter mistake could waste enormous computational resources.

But scale also brought unexpected benefits. The model had a kind of "general intelligence" that I hadn't seen in specialized vision models. It could reason about concepts it had never explicitly seen in the training data. This was both exciting and slightly unnerving.

The Art of Prompting

Another revelation was the importance of prompt engineering. In vision models, you feed in an image and get a prediction. With LLMs, the way you phrase your query dramatically affects the output.

Fine-tuning isn't just about the weights—it's about shaping the space of possible interactions.

We spent considerable time crafting our training data to model the kinds of interactions we wanted. Questions about TCM diagnoses, herb combinations, treatment principles. Each example was teaching the model not just facts, but a way of reasoning.

Knowledge Base Integration

One of the most interesting aspects was integrating an external knowledge base. The LLaMA model provided the reasoning capability, but we augmented it with a structured database of TCM knowledge.

This retrieval-augmented approach gave us the best of both worlds: the fluent, contextual responses of a language model with the accuracy of a curated database. When the model wasn't sure about a specific fact, it could look it up.

Looking Forward

This experience planted a seed that would grow into my later interest in Mixture-of-Experts. Watching how the language model seemed to activate different "modes" depending on the query made me curious about more explicit expert routing mechanisms.

I'm still fascinated by LLMs, though my current work has returned to vision. But the lessons about scale, interaction design, and hybrid architectures continue to influence my thinking.
