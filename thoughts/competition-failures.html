Learning from Competition Failures

Competition Learning

Before winning the road damage detection competition, we failed spectacularly in two others. Those failures taught me more than the win ever could. Looking back, I'm almost grateful for those early defeats—they forced us to examine our assumptions and develop a more principled approach.

Failure #1: Overengineering

In our first competition, we built a complex ensemble of models. We had attention mechanisms, multi-scale feature fusion, custom loss functions, and a elaborate post-processing pipeline. We were convinced that more complexity meant better results.

We finished in the middle of the pack. Teams with simpler approaches beat us handily.

The lesson: complexity is not a virtue in itself. Every added component is another potential point of failure, another hyperparameter to tune, another source of bugs. A well-tuned simple model often beats a poorly-tuned complex one.

Failure #2: Ignoring the Data

For the second competition, we learned our lesson about complexity—or so we thought. We used a standard architecture and focused on training techniques. But we still finished poorly.

What we missed: the data had specific characteristics that we never properly analyzed. We spent weeks on architecture when careful data augmentation would have yielded better results. The winning teams had invested heavily in understanding and preprocessing the data.

Sometimes the boring solution is the right one.

The Road Damage Win

For the road damage detection competition, we took a radically different approach. YOLOv8 baseline—nothing fancy. Aggressive but targeted augmentation based on careful analysis of the training data. And then we let the GPU hours do the heavy lifting: 10,000 samples, 150 epochs on 4× NVIDIA 4090s.

We won the National Second Prize.

The approach was almost embarrassingly simple compared to our earlier attempts. But that simplicity was hard-won—it came from understanding what actually matters in these competitions.

Lessons Learned

Start simple. Understand your data before you write a single line of model code. Invest in proper experimentation infrastructure so you can iterate quickly. And most importantly, be willing to throw away elegant solutions that don't work in favor of ugly ones that do.

The experience also taught me something about collaboration. Working with Hancang and Shuxian, we developed a rhythm: one person would focus on data, another on training, another on evaluation. This division of labor was crucial—it let us move fast without stepping on each other's toes.

I still fail at things regularly. But now I fail faster, learn more from each failure, and move on with better intuitions. That's progress.
